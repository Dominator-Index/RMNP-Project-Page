TL;DR: Replace Muon's Newton–Schulz iteration with a single row-wise $\ell_2$ normalization. Same matrix-level adaptivity, same convergence guarantees, but preconditioning drops from $\mathcal{O}(mn\cdot\min(m,n))$ to $\mathcal{O}(mn)$ — a 13–44× wall-clock speedup.

Abstract

Preconditioned adaptive methods have gained significant attention for training deep neural networks, as they capture rich curvature information of the loss landscape. The central challenge lies in balancing preconditioning effectiveness with the computational efficiency of implementing the preconditioner. Among recent advances, Muon stands out by using Newton–Schulz iteration to obtain preconditioned updates without explicitly constructing the preconditioning matrix. Despite its advantages, the efficiency of Muon still leaves room for improvement.

We introduce RMNP (Row-Momentum Normalized Preconditioning), an optimizer that replaces Newton–Schulz iteration with a simple row-wise ($d_{\text{in}}$) $\ell_2$ normalization, motivated by the empirically observed diagonal block structure of the Transformer layer-wise Hessian. We empirically verify that orthogonalization and row-wise $\ell_2$ normalization are asymptotically equivalent in the Transformer case. This substitution reduces the per-iteration complexity from $\mathcal{O}(mn\cdot\min(m,n))$ to $\mathcal{O}(mn)$ for an $m\times n$ weight matrix while maintaining comparable optimization performance. Theoretically, we establish non-convex convergence guarantees that match recent results for Muon and achieve the minimax-optimal complexity. Extensive experiments on large language model pretraining show that RMNP delivers competitive performance while substantially reducing preconditioning wall-clock time.

Method

Recent work shows the layer-wise Hessian of Transformers is row-wise block-diagonal dominant: diagonal blocks (interactions within the same row) are far larger than off-diagonal blocks. By Lemma 4 of Shampoo, the Muon preconditioner takes the form $H_{\text{MUON}} = (V_t V_t^\top)^{1/2} \otimes I_n$. Retaining only the dominant diagonal blocks yields the RMNP preconditioner:

$$ H_{\text{RMNP}} = \left(\operatorname{diag}(V_t V_t^\top)\right)^{1/2} \otimes I_n. $$

The resulting preconditioned update collapses to a plain row-wise $\ell_2$ normalization of the momentum matrix:

$$ \left[\left(\operatorname{diag}(V_t V_t^\top)\right)^{-1/2} V_t\right]_{i,:} = \frac{V_{t,i:}}{\sqrt{(V_t V_t^\top)_{ii}}} = \frac{V_{t,i:}}{\lVert V_{t,i:}\rVert_{\ell_2}}. $$

Muon

  1. $G_t \leftarrow \nabla f(W_t; \xi^t)$
  2. $V_t \leftarrow \beta V_{t-1} + (1-\beta) G_t$
  3. $D_t \leftarrow \mathrm{NS}_5(V_t) = (V_tV_t^\top)^{-1/2} V_t$
  4. $W_{t+1} \leftarrow W_t - \eta_t D_t$

RMNP (ours)

  1. $G_t \leftarrow \nabla f(W_t; \xi^t)$
  2. $V_t \leftarrow \beta V_{t-1} + (1-\beta) G_t$
  3. $D_t \leftarrow \mathrm{RN}(V_t) = (\operatorname{diag}(V_tV_t^\top))^{-1/2} V_t$
  4. $W_{t+1} \leftarrow W_t - \eta_t D_t$

The two algorithms differ in a single line: full orthogonalization (Newton–Schulz) becomes row normalization.

Why it works: diagonal dominance of the Muon preconditioner

RMNP's approximation is valid exactly when the Gram matrix $V_t V_t^\top$ is diagonally dominant. We track the per-row ratio of the diagonal entry to the average off-diagonal magnitude across training. Across architectures and scales, the minimum ratio $\overline{r}_{\min}$ stays above $1$, the average $\overline{r}_{\text{avg}}$ exceeds $5$, and the maximum reaches the tens — and dominance strengthens with model size.

Convergence guarantees

Under standard non-convex assumptions, RMNP matches the best-known Muon guarantees. Under $\lVert\cdot\rVert_F$-smoothness it attains $\mathcal{O}(m^2 L_F \sigma^2 \Delta \epsilon^{-4})$; under the matched $\lVert\cdot\rVert_{\infty,2}$-smoothness it improves to $\mathcal{O}(m L_{\infty,2} \sigma^2 \Delta \epsilon^{-4})$ — a quadratic improvement in dimension dependence, mirroring Muon's gain under nuclear-norm geometry.
